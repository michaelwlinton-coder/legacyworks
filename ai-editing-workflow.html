Level 1 — Beginner
CapCut: your first edited video

You just finished your first Riverside session. You have a raw video file that's 45 minutes long, full of pauses, "umms," and moments where nothing happens. This section takes you from that raw file to a polished 15–20 minute episode — using CapCut's AI tools to do most of the work for you.

🎯 Goal: A watchable, captioned 15–20 min episode ready to upload to YouTube
1
Download and open CapCut

Go to capcut.com on your computer and download the free desktop app. Install it like any other program — open the downloaded file, click through the installer, and launch CapCut. It's free with no credit card required.

If you prefer to edit on your phone, download the CapCut app from the App Store or Google Play. The phone version works identically.

💡
Desktop is easier for this

While CapCut works on phones, editing a 45-minute interview is much easier on a desktop or laptop where you have a larger screen and a keyboard.

2
Start a new project and import your recording

Open CapCut and click "New Project". A window will appear asking you to add media. Click "Import" or drag your video file from your downloads folder directly into the window.

Your Riverside recording is usually saved in your Downloads folder or on your Desktop. It will be a file ending in .mp4 or .mov. Drag it in or click to find it.

3
Add the video to your timeline

After importing, your video appears in the top left panel. Click the "+" button on it, or drag it down to the timeline at the bottom of the screen. The timeline is the long horizontal strip — this is where all your editing happens. You should now see your video as a colored bar in the timeline.

4
Use Smart Cut AI to remove all silence automatically

This is the single most powerful thing CapCut can do for you. In the left sidebar, look for "Smart Tools" or click on your video in the timeline and look for "Smart Cut" in the top toolbar. Click it.

CapCut will analyze your entire video and automatically find and remove all the silent gaps — the pauses where nobody is talking, the moments where the interviewee is thinking, the dead air at the beginning and end. This alone cuts most 45-minute interviews down to 25–30 minutes.

Before Smart Cut
"Well... [4 second pause] my grandmother... [2 seconds] she was born in [long pause] 1932..."
After Smart Cut
"Well... my grandmother... she was born in 1932..."
⚠️
Review before accepting

Smart Cut sometimes removes meaningful pauses — the 3-second silence after an emotional answer is part of the story. Before clicking "Apply," review the suggested cuts and uncheck any pauses that feel important to keep.

5
Manually trim the beginning and end

Find the very beginning of your timeline. Before your subject actually starts talking, there's usually 30–60 seconds of setup — you saying "okay let's start" or your subject adjusting their camera. Hover over the left edge of your video clip in the timeline until you see a double-headed arrow, then drag it to the right to trim off that dead time.

Do the same at the end — trim off any time after the conversation naturally ends.

6
Add auto-captions in one click

In the left sidebar, click "Captions" then "Auto Captions". Select the language (English) and click "Start". CapCut will transcribe every word spoken in your video and place animated subtitles on screen.

This takes 2–5 minutes for a 30-minute video. When it's done, captions appear as blocks on a separate track in your timeline. Family members who are hard of hearing can now read along. And families who share the video with relatives who don't speak the same language can read it too.

💡
Fix any caption errors

AI transcription is 90–95% accurate. Click on any caption block in the timeline to edit the text. Names, places, and unusual words are the most likely to need fixing. Spend 5 minutes reviewing — it's worth it.

7
Add a simple title at the beginning

Click "Text" in the left sidebar, then "Default Text". A text box appears on your video. Type something like: "Mom's Story — Week 1: Where We Came From".

Drag this text clip in the timeline to the very beginning so it appears for the first 5 seconds. Change the font and color to something clean — white text on the dark background of your video works perfectly.

8
Export your finished episode

Click the "Export" button in the top right corner. Use these exact settings:

Click "Export" and wait. A 30-minute video takes about 10–15 minutes to export. Do not close CapCut while it's exporting.

9
Upload to your family's private YouTube channel

Once exported, go to youtube.com, sign in to the family account, and click the "+" icon or "Upload video". Select your exported file. While it uploads, fill in:

Title: "Week 1 — Roots & Origins — [Name]'s Story"
Description: A brief sentence about what this session covered
Visibility: Click "Unlisted" (only people with the link can watch) or "Private" (only people you specifically invite)

🎉
You just produced your first episode

Share the YouTube link with your family. Their reaction to hearing their parent or grandparent's voice on screen for the first time is something you'll never forget.

Level 2 — Intermediate
Descript: edit words, cut video

Descript is the most powerful thing that's ever happened to non-editors. Instead of scrubbing through video looking for the right moment, you read the transcript like a document and delete the parts you don't want. The video cuts itself. This section teaches you to edit faster, cleaner, and with more creative control than CapCut alone.

🎯 Goal: Professional, tightly edited episodes with clean audio — in half the time
1
Create your free Descript account

Go to descript.com and click "Get started free". Sign up with your email. The free plan gives you 1 hour of transcription per month — enough for 1–2 sessions. When you're ready to process more, upgrade to the $24/month Creator plan for unlimited transcription.

2
Create a new project and import your Riverside recording

Click "New Project" then "Import file". Navigate to your Riverside recording and select it. Descript will upload the video and begin transcribing it automatically — this takes 3–8 minutes depending on video length.

While it transcribes, you'll see a progress bar. When it's done, you'll see your video on the left and a full text transcript on the right.

3
Understand the Descript interface

The Descript screen has two main parts:

Left side: Your video preview — you can watch your footage here
Right side: Your transcript — every word spoken, in order, highlighted as the video plays

The magic: whatever you do to the transcript happens to the video. Delete a sentence of text — that sentence disappears from the video. It's that simple.

ℹ️
The most important thing to understand

You are NOT editing video. You are editing a document. Think of it like deleting paragraphs from a Word document — except those paragraphs are video. This is why Descript is so powerful for people who've never edited video before.

4
Remove filler words in one click

Go to Edit → Remove filler words. Descript will find every "um," "uh," "like," "you know," and "sort of" in your video and highlight them. Review the list and click "Remove all" or go through them one by one. This alone makes your subject sound 40% more articulate and confident on screen.

Raw transcript
"So, um, my grandfather was, uh, you know, he was like a farmer and, um, I think..."
After filler removal
"So, my grandfather was a farmer and I think..."
5
Delete the parts you don't want — just like a document

Read through the transcript like you're reading an article. When you see a section that doesn't add anything — a false start, a long digression, a moment where the subject went off-topic — just highlight that text and press Delete on your keyboard. That part of the video is instantly removed.

You're looking to cut:
• False starts ("I was going to say — actually, never mind...")
• Long digressions that go nowhere
• Technical moments ("hold on, my phone is ringing")
• Anything longer than 30 seconds that doesn't add to the story

⚠️
You can always undo

Press Cmd+Z (Mac) or Ctrl+Z (Windows) to undo any cut. Don't be afraid to experiment — nothing is permanent until you export.

6
Apply Studio Sound to clean the audio

This is Descript's most impressive feature. Click on your video clip, then find "Studio Sound" in the right panel (or go to Effects → Studio Sound). Turn it on with one click.

Studio Sound uses AI to remove background noise, reduce echo, equalize the voice, and make your subject sound like they recorded in a professional studio — even if they were in their kitchen. The difference is remarkable.

💡
A/B test before committing

Toggle Studio Sound on and off while the video plays to hear the difference. If it makes the voice sound slightly robotic, reduce the "Enhancement level" slider to 70% instead of 100%.

7
Add captions from the transcript

Since Descript already transcribed your video, adding captions is instant. Go to Captions → Add captions. The captions are automatically synced to the words in your video because they come from the same transcript you just edited.

Choose a caption style — the default "Clean" style (white text, dark background) works well. You can customize font, size, and position in the right panel.

8
Export your finished video

Click "Export" in the top right. Choose "Video" as the format. Use these settings:

SettingValueWhy
Resolution1080pFull HD — crisp on any screen
FormatMP4Universal — plays everywhere
QualityHighWorth the larger file
CaptionsBurned inAlways visible, no separate file needed

Click Export and wait. Upload the finished file to your YouTube family channel.

Level 3 — Expert
Cinematic quality every time

You've edited 10+ sessions. You know the basics. Now you want your episodes to look and sound like something from a documentary series — not just "good for a home video." These techniques are what separate a polished legacy film from a recorded Zoom call.

🎯 Goal: Documentary-quality episodes that look like they were made by professionals
1
Use Riverside AI's Magic Clips to find the best moments first

Before editing the full session, go back to Riverside and click "Magic Clips" on your recording. Riverside's AI reads the transcript and automatically identifies the most emotionally resonant, most interesting, and most shareable moments — usually 3–8 clips of 30 seconds to 3 minutes each.

Watch these clips first. They tell you where the heart of the session is. Then build your full edit around those moments — don't bury them in the middle, start with the most compelling clip as your cold open.

✨
The cold open technique

Start your episode with 60 seconds of the most emotional or surprising moment from the session — before the title card. This hooks family members immediately and makes them want to watch the whole thing. Documentaries do this. Now you do too.

2
Build a three-act structure for every episode

Even a 20-minute interview episode needs structure. The best documentary episodes follow this pattern:

Act 1 — Hook (0:00–2:00): The most compelling clip. Drops the viewer directly into the story.
Title card (2:00–2:10): Episode name, week number, date.
Act 2 — The story (2:10–17:00): The interview in chronological order. This is the bulk of the episode.
Act 3 — The resonant close (17:00–20:00): End on something meaningful — a piece of wisdom, a moment of emotion, or a line that will stay with the viewer.

3
Add B-roll — family photos woven into the story

B-roll is footage or images that play over the interview audio while you hear the subject's voice but don't see their face. In documentary filmmaking, this is how you bring a story to life visually.

For family stories, your B-roll is family photos. When your subject says "My father worked on the railroad" — cut to an old photo of their father. When they describe their childhood home — show a photo of it.

In CapCut or Descript: import your family photos, drag them above the interview video in the timeline, and set them to appear at the exact moment the subject mentions them. 3–5 seconds per photo is ideal. The result feels like Ken Burns.

✨
The Ken Burns effect

In CapCut, select a photo in your timeline, click "Animation," and apply a slow zoom-in or pan effect. This gently moving still image over spoken audio is one of the most emotionally effective techniques in documentary filmmaking. Ken Burns has used it for 40 years. Now so do you.

4
Add background music — very quietly

Subtle music transforms the emotional feel of an episode. The key word is subtle — if the viewer can clearly hear the music, it's too loud. Music should be felt, not heard.

Where to find free music:
• YouTube Audio Library (free, no copyright issues): studio.youtube.com → Audio Library
• Pixabay Music (pixabay.com/music) — free, licensed for any use
• CapCut's built-in library — search "gentle" or "nostalgic"

Music settings: Import the music track, place it below your video in the timeline. Set the volume to 5–10% of your voice track. Test by watching 30 seconds — you should barely be aware the music is there.

Consistency across episodes

Once you find color settings that look great, save them as a preset in CapCut. Apply the same preset to every episode so your series has a consistent, professional visual identity.

6
Create a 60-second highlight clip for sharing

Every episode should have a short shareable version — 60 seconds or less — that you can share on iMessage, WhatsApp, or send to family members who might not watch the full episode.

Use Riverside's Magic Clips to find the single best 60-second moment. Export it as a vertical 9:16 video (portrait orientation) for phones, or keep it at 16:9 for horizontal sharing. Add a simple caption at the bottom — just the subject's name and the year.

This 60-second clip is what gets sent to cousins, shown at family dinners, and shared on birthdays. It's the gateway drug that makes everyone want to watch the full episode.

The things that make the real difference

These are the principles that professional documentary editors use. Apply them and your episodes will feel different from anything your family has ever seen.

01
Always end on the best line — not the last line

The last thing people hear is what they remember. Scan your entire transcript for the single most powerful sentence your subject said. End there, even if it happened in the middle of the session. Cut everything that came after it.

02
Cut to the next shot 0.5 seconds early

When cutting between clips or photos, trim the end of the outgoing clip by half a second. This creates a snappy, confident rhythm that makes editing feel professional. Viewers never notice — they just feel the pace is right.

03
The quieter the music, the more emotional the impact

Counterintuitively, turning music down makes it more powerful. When the viewer has to lean in to notice the music, it works on them subconsciously. If they can hear it clearly, it becomes a distraction from the voice — which is the only thing that matters.

04
Keep every raw file forever — and back it up twice

Never delete your original Riverside recordings. Storage is cheap; your grandfather's voice is irreplaceable. Keep a copy on your computer, one on Google Drive or iCloud, and one on an external hard drive. Name the files clearly: "Week_01_Roots_RAW_2026-01-14.mp4"

05
Edit when you're emotionally neutral — not right after recording

You just spent 45 minutes listening to your mother's childhood. You're emotional. Don't edit right now — you'll either cut too much (trying to protect yourself from the hard parts) or too little (keeping everything because it all feels precious). Wait a day, then edit with fresh eyes.

06
Watch your edit once all the way through before exporting

Every time. No exceptions. Your ears catch things your eyes miss when you're in editing mode. Watch it like a family member watching it for the first time. The moment you cringe or get bored — that's the part that needs one more cut.
