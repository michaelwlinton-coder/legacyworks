52 sessions.
One story.

You've recorded a year of interviews. Now you have hours of footage — and the hardest question of all: how do you turn it into something your family will actually watch? This template gives you the structure. It's the same framework professional documentary editors use to shape a life into a story.

The problem this solves

A pile of raw interviews is an archive, not a story. Nobody sits down to watch 20 hours of footage. The difference between a recording that gets watched once and a film that gets watched for generations is structure. This template shows you exactly how to organize what you captured into something that holds people's attention from beginning to end.

Three ways to assemble your footage

You don't have to do all three. Pick the one that fits your family — or do the weekly episodes as you go and assemble the highlight film at the end.

📺
Weekly Episodes
Easiest · Do it as you go · 52 short films

Edit each session into its own 15–20 minute episode, one per week, and upload them to your family's private YouTube channel. This is the recommended approach for most families. It's manageable, it spreads the work out, and your family gets to watch the story unfold in real time over the year.

🎬
The Highlight Film
Most cinematic · Assemble at the end · 30–60 min

After all 52 sessions are recorded, pull the single best moment from each chapter and weave them into one 30–60 minute film that captures the whole arc of their life. This is the version you screen at the 80th birthday. It takes the most work but creates the most powerful single artifact.

📖
Chapter Films
A middle path · 12 themed films

Group the 52 sessions into their 12 life chapters and edit one film per chapter — "Childhood," "Love & Marriage," "Legacy," and so on. This gives you 12 substantial 20–30 minute films organized by theme, which families love for revisiting specific parts of a life.

The life arc — how to order a life

Whether you're making weekly episodes or a single highlight film, the underlying structure is the same. A life story, like any story, has acts. Here's how the 12 chapters map onto a classic narrative arc.

The four-act life structure
Act 1 — The Foundation
Where they came from
Chapters 1–3: Roots & Origins · Childhood · Family
Establishes who they are and the world that made them. This is the "once upon a time."
Act 2 — The Becoming
Who they chose to be
Chapters 4–7: Coming of Age · Love & Marriage · Work & Purpose · Parenthood
The decisions, the love, the work, the family. The bulk of a life — and the bulk of your film.
Act 3 — The Depths
What tested and shaped them
Chapters 8–9: Faith & Belief · Hardship & Resilience
Every meaningful story has a darkness. This is where the character's depth is revealed.
Act 4 — The Resolution
What they learned and leave behind
Chapters 10–12: Joy & Gratitude · Wisdom · Legacy
The story comes to rest on meaning. This is the emotional payoff — and where you end.
💡
Why this order works

It moves from the external (where they came from) to the internal (what they believe and what they've learned). A story that ends on wisdom and legacy leaves the viewer with meaning — not just facts. Always save the most emotionally resonant material for the end.

The single episode template

Every weekly episode should follow this internal structure. Whether the episode is 10 minutes or 25, these segments keep it watchable from start to finish.

SegmentWhat goes hereLength
1 Cold open
The single most compelling moment from this session — drop the viewer right into it before anything else.		 30–60 sec
2 Title card
"[Name]'s Story · Week [X] · [Chapter title]" on a clean background.		 5–10 sec
3 The conversation
The interview itself, edited tight. Filler removed, silences trimmed, off-topic moments cut. Family photos woven in as B-roll where relevant.		 10–20 min
4 The resonant close
End on the most meaningful line from the session — a piece of wisdom, an emotional moment, a perfect sentence. Even if it happened mid-session, move it here.		 30–90 sec
5 End card
"LegacyWorks · Every life remembered" + optional teaser for next week's chapter.		 5–10 sec

Your episode planner

Before you edit each session, fill this out. Five minutes of planning saves an hour of aimless editing. Print one of these for every session.

Episode Planning Sheet
Week ______ · Chapter ____________________ · Date recorded ____________
1. The cold open — what's the single best moment?
Write the timestamp and a one-line description of the most gripping 60 seconds...
Look for: the most surprising thing they said, the most emotional moment, or the funniest story.
2. The through-line — what is this episode really about?
In one sentence: this episode is about...
Every episode needs a single theme. If you can't name it in one sentence, the edit will feel scattered.
3. The cuts — what 3–5 things should be removed?
Timestamps of digressions, false starts, or dead moments to cut...
Be ruthless. Anything that doesn't serve the through-line should go.
4. The photos — which family photos belong here?
List photos to add as B-roll and where they go...
When they mention a person, place, or event — show a photo of it if you have one.
5. The closing line — what's the perfect ending?
The single best sentence to end on (timestamp + the words)...
This is the most important decision. The last line is what the viewer carries with them.

⚠️
Resist including everything

The hardest part of the highlight film is leaving things out. You'll want to keep every precious moment — but a 3-hour film won't get watched, and a tight 45-minute film will be watched a hundred times. Keep the full episodes for the archive. Make the highlight film ruthlessly short.

Six storytelling principles

These are the rules professional documentary editors live by. Apply them and your film will feel like a story, not a recording.

01
Emotion over chronology

When in doubt, order moments by emotional impact, not by date. A film that builds to its most powerful moment beats one that just lists events in order.

02
Show, then tell

When they mention a photo, a place, or a person — show it. The viewer should see what the storyteller is describing whenever possible. This is what B-roll is for.

03
One theme per episode

Every episode should be about one thing. If a session wandered across five topics, consider splitting it into two focused episodes rather than one scattered one.

04
Start strong, end stronger

The first 30 seconds decide whether someone keeps watching. The last 30 seconds decide whether they remember it. Spend the most editing care on these two moments.

05
Leave space for silence

Not every pause is dead air. The silence after someone says something profound is part of the story. Cut the empty silences; keep the meaningful ones.

06
Let them be the narrator

Resist the urge to add your own voiceover explaining things. The most powerful life films are told entirely in the subject's own voice. Their words are enough.

📁
Name files clearly and consistently

Use a format like Week_03_Family_EDITED_2026-02-04.mp4. When you're assembling the highlight film 11 months from now, you'll thank yourself for being able to find exactly what you need instantly.
